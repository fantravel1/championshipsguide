Esports Championships

Competitive gaming has become a global spectacle. From the League of Legends World Championship to The International, esports championships now rival traditional sports in viewership and prize money.

Context

The Rise of Esports

From basement LAN parties to sold-out arenas and billion-dollar industries, competitive gaming has undergone one of the most dramatic transformations in the history of entertainment and sport.

Esports — organized, competitive video gaming — has grown from a niche subculture into a global phenomenon that commands audiences rivaling traditional sports. What began in the early 2000s with small LAN tournaments in internet cafes has evolved into a multi-billion-dollar industry with professional leagues, franchised teams, and world championships held in the same stadiums that host the NBA Finals and Champions League matches.

The numbers tell the story of a meteoric rise. Global esports viewership now exceeds 500 million unique viewers annually, with the most popular events drawing tens of millions of concurrent viewers — numbers that surpass the audience for many traditional sporting events. The League of Legends World Championship regularly attracts over 30 million peak concurrent viewers. Global esports revenue has surpassed $1.8 billion, driven by media rights, sponsorships, merchandise, and ticket sales.

The path toward mainstream recognition has accelerated in recent years. Esports was featured as an official medal event at the 2023 Asian Games in Hangzhou, China, marking the first time competitive gaming was part of a major multi-sport event recognized by the International Olympic Committee. Countries including South Korea, China, Germany, and France have recognized esports athletes as professional sportspeople. Major traditional sports organizations — from the NBA (NBA 2K League) to Formula 1 (F1 Esports Series) — have launched their own esports divisions.

The infrastructure supporting esports has matured rapidly. Purpose-built esports arenas have opened in cities like Seoul, Los Angeles, Berlin, and Shanghai. Training facilities rival those of professional sports teams, with coaches, analysts, sports psychologists, and nutritionists supporting players who train 8-12 hours per day. University scholarship programs for esports have proliferated, and esports curricula are offered at institutions worldwide.

Esports in the ChampionshipsGuide Tier System

ChampionshipsGuide rates esports championships using the same tier system applied to traditional sports, evaluating them on global audience, competitive depth, historical prestige, and cultural significance. The largest esports championships — including the League of Legends World Championship, The International, and CS2 Majors — are rated Tier 2 (Elite), placing them alongside events like the Stanley Cup, the World Series, and the Tour de France. Other major esports championships are rated Tier 3 (Major), reflecting their significant but more specialized audiences. As the industry continues to grow, these ratings may evolve.

Elite Esports Championships

These are the most prestigious and widely watched esports championships in the world. They command massive global audiences, carry decades of competitive history, and define the pinnacle of their respective games.

Tier 2

Esports — MOBA

League of Legends World Championship (Worlds)

The biggest esports event on the planet. Organized by Riot Games, the League of Legends World Championship — universally known as "Worlds" — is the annual culmination of a year-long competitive season that spans every major region on Earth.

Teams qualify through regional leagues: the LCK (Korea), LPL (China), LEC (Europe), LCS (North America), and several other emerging regions. Korean and Chinese teams have historically dominated, with organizations like T1 (led by the legendary Faker), Gen.G, and JDG among the most successful in tournament history.

Held annually from October to November, Worlds follows a format that progresses from a Play-In Stage through a Group Stage (or Swiss Stage) to a single-elimination knockout bracket. The 2023 World Championship final was held at the Gocheok Sky Dome in Seoul, South Korea, in front of a roaring crowd and over 30 million peak concurrent viewers worldwide — making it one of the most-watched sporting events of any kind that year.

The Summoner's Cup is one of the most recognizable trophies in esports, and winning Worlds is the ultimate achievement for any League of Legends player or team.

🎮 MOBA 📅 Oct-Nov (annual) 🌐 Global
Tier 2

Esports — MOBA

The International (Dota 2)

Valve's flagship Dota 2 championship is one of the most iconic events in esports history, famous for its record-breaking, community-crowdfunded prize pools. The International (TI) once offered a total prize pool exceeding $40 million — by far the largest in esports history — funded through Valve's Battle Pass system, where a percentage of player purchases contributed directly to the pool.

Teams from around the world compete in a grueling tournament that combines a group stage with a double-elimination bracket, producing some of the most dramatic comebacks and upsets in competitive gaming. The European organization OG made history by winning back-to-back Internationals in 2018 and 2019, a feat many considered impossible in such a volatile and competitive game.

While the prize pool model has evolved in recent years, The International remains the aspirational goal of every Dota 2 professional. Its legacy is built on decade-plus of competition, passionate fans, and the game's extraordinary strategic depth — often compared to chess in its complexity.

🎮 MOBA 📅 Varies (annual) 💰 Largest prize pool in esports history
Tier 2

Esports — Tactical FPS

CS2 Major Championships

Counter-Strike's Major Championships are the most prestigious events in competitive first-person shooter history. Valve sponsors two Majors per year, each offering a $1.25 million prize pool and the most coveted trophies in CS history.

The Counter-Strike competitive scene has endured for over two decades, transitioning from CS 1.6 through CS:GO to the current Counter-Strike 2 (CS2). This longevity has produced legendary teams and storylines. Organizations like Natus Vincere (Na'Vi), FaZe Clan, Team Vitality, Astralis, and Cloud9 have written their names into esports history through Major victories.

Majors are held in large arenas across Europe and beyond — from the PGL Major in Copenhagen to events in Paris, Antwerp, Rio de Janeiro, and Stockholm. The atmosphere at a CS2 Major final is electric, with thousands of fans chanting and the tension of a single elimination bracket creating moments that define careers. The in-game sticker economy — where fans purchase virtual stickers of their favorite teams and players — generates millions in additional revenue.

Tier 3 — Major

Major Esports Championships

These championships represent the pinnacle of competition in their respective games. While they may have smaller global audiences than the Tier 2 events, they are deeply significant within their communities and attract millions of dedicated fans.

Tier 3

Valorant Champions

Riot Games' tactical FPS championship has grown explosively since Valorant's 2020 launch. The Valorant Champions Tour (VCT) culminates in the Champions event, bringing together the best teams from international leagues spanning the Americas, EMEA (Europe, Middle East, and Africa), the Pacific, and China. With its blend of precise gunplay and unique agent abilities, Valorant has attracted top talent from CS, Overwatch, and other FPS titles, and its viewership numbers continue to climb year over year.

🎮 Tactical FPS 📅 August-September (annual)
Tier 3

Call of Duty League Championship

The CDL Grand Finals crown the champion of the franchised Call of Duty League. Twelve city-based teams — including the Atlanta FaZe, OpTic Texas, and LA Thieves — compete across a regular season and playoffs in the latest annual Call of Duty title. The CDL carries the legacy of one of gaming's most iconic franchises and has a devoted fanbase, particularly in North America. The championship weekend features intense best-of-series matches in front of passionate live crowds.

🎮 FPS 📅 July-August (annual)
Tier 3

Overwatch Champions Series

Following the conclusion of the Overwatch League (OWL), Blizzard Entertainment restructured competitive Overwatch into the Overwatch Champions Series (OWCS). The new format moves away from the franchised league model toward an open circuit that allows more teams to compete. The championship brings together the top Overwatch 2 teams from regional tournaments across North America, EMEA, Asia, and other regions, preserving the fast-paced, team-based hero shooter competition that made OWL a pioneer in esports.

🎮 Hero Shooter 📅 Varies (annual)
Tier 3

Fortnite Champion Series

Epic Games' Fortnite Champion Series (FNCS) is the premier competitive event for one of the most popular games in the world. The FNCS features solo, duo, and trio formats that test building mechanics, aim, and strategic decision-making in a battle royale environment. Epic has invested heavily in competitive Fortnite, awarding tens of millions of dollars in prize money across multiple seasons. The 2019 Fortnite World Cup, where teenager Kyle "Bugha" Giersdorf won $3 million, remains one of the most iconic moments in esports history.

🎮 Battle Royale 📅 Multiple seasons (annual)
Tier 3

PUBG Global Championship

The PUBG Global Championship (PGC) is the world championship of PlayerUnknown's Battlegrounds, the game that popularized the battle royale genre. Teams from regional leagues across the Americas, Europe, Asia Pacific, and other regions compete for the title of world champion. PUBG esports has a particularly strong following in Korea, China, and Southeast Asia, and the PGC offers one of the largest prize pools in the battle royale esports scene. The game's tactical depth and squad-based gameplay make for compelling competitive viewing.

🎮 Battle Royale 📅 November-December (annual)
Tier 3

Rocket League World Championship

The Rocket League Championship Series (RLCS) World Championship crowns the best team in one of the most unique esports in existence — a game that combines rocket-powered cars with soccer. Developed by Psyonix (now owned by Epic Games), Rocket League's easy-to-understand concept and incredible mechanical skill ceiling have made it one of the most accessible and entertaining esports to watch. The RLCS features teams from North America, Europe, South America, the Middle East and North Africa, Asia Pacific, and Sub-Saharan Africa.

🎮 Sports/Racing 📅 June-July (annual)
Tier 3

Rainbow Six Siege Major / Six Invitational

Ubisoft's Rainbow Six Siege has cultivated one of the most dedicated competitive communities in esports. The Six Invitational is the annual world championship, bringing together the top teams from regional leagues across the globe. Siege's emphasis on destructible environments, operator selection, and tactical planning creates a spectator experience unlike any other FPS esport. The game's competitive scene has thrived for nearly a decade, with strong followings in Brazil, Europe, and North America. Multiple Majors throughout the year complement the Invitational as the circuit's crown jewel.

🎮 Tactical FPS 📅 February (Six Invitational)
Tier 3

EA Sports FC Pro World Championship

Formerly known as the FIFA eWorld Cup, the EA Sports FC Pro World Championship is the pinnacle of competitive virtual football. Players compete through an online qualification system and regional playoffs to earn a spot at the World Championship. The event carries the backing of both EA Sports and FIFA (now operating independently), giving it a unique crossover appeal between the esports and traditional sports audiences. Top players compete for significant prize money and the prestige of being crowned the best virtual footballer in the world.

🎮 Sports Simulation 📅 Varies (annual)
Tier 3

StarCraft II World Championship Series

The StarCraft II WCS holds a special place in esports history. StarCraft was the game that built the esports industry in South Korea, where professional gamers became national celebrities and matches were broadcast on dedicated television channels. While Blizzard officially concluded the WCS circuit, the legacy of StarCraft II as a competitive title endures through community-organized events and the game's continued popularity in Korea. The WCS Global Finals produced some of the most technically brilliant and emotionally charged matches in esports history, showcasing the extraordinary individual skill required in real-time strategy competition.

Fighting Games

Fighting Game Championships

The Fighting Game Community (FGC) is one of the oldest and most passionate subcultures in competitive gaming. Unlike most esports, the FGC has grassroots origins rooted in arcade culture, local tournaments, and a fiercely independent community spirit.

FGC

EVO (Evolution Championship Series)

EVO is the Super Bowl of fighting games. Held annually in Las Vegas (and more recently with a companion event, EVO Japan), the Evolution Championship Series is the largest and most prestigious fighting game tournament in the world. Thousands of players from around the globe compete across multiple game titles — including Street Fighter, Tekken, Guilty Gear, Mortal Kombat, and more — in open-bracket tournaments where anyone can enter.

What makes EVO unique in the esports landscape is its open format. Unlike franchised leagues where only contracted teams compete, EVO is open to anyone who registers. A complete unknown can enter the bracket and, through sheer skill, defeat the world's top-ranked players. This grassroots, meritocratic ethos is central to EVO's identity and the broader FGC culture.

EVO has produced some of the most iconic moments in esports history, including the legendary "Daigo Parry" at EVO 2004 — a full-parry comeback in Street Fighter III: 3rd Strike that is considered by many to be the single greatest moment in competitive gaming history. Now owned by Sony Interactive Entertainment, EVO continues to grow while maintaining the community-driven spirit that defines the FGC.

🎮 Multi-Game FGC 📅 July (annual) 🎲 Open bracket
Tier 3

Capcom Cup

The Capcom Cup is the official world championship for Street Fighter, the franchise that helped define the fighting game genre. Players qualify through the Capcom Pro Tour (CPT), a year-long circuit of premier and ranking events held around the world. The top qualifiers earn their spot at the Capcom Cup finals, where they compete for the title of Street Fighter world champion and a substantial prize pool. With the launch of Street Fighter 6, the competitive scene has experienced a significant resurgence in both participation and viewership, bringing new energy to one of gaming's most storied competitive franchises.

🎮 Street Fighter 📅 February (annual)
Tier 3

Tekken World Tour Finals

The Tekken World Tour (TWT) Finals is the culmination of Bandai Namco's global competitive circuit for the Tekken franchise. Players earn points through a series of Master, Challenger, and Dojo events worldwide, with the top-ranked players qualifying for the TWT Finals. Tekken's 3D fighting system, deep character roster, and emphasis on mind games and punishment have made it one of the most mechanically demanding fighting games. The franchise has a massive following in Korea, Japan, Pakistan, and increasingly across the globe, and the release of Tekken 8 has reinvigorated the competitive scene.

🎮 Tekken 📅 Varies (annual)

The Culture of the FGC

The Fighting Game Community (FGC) has a distinct identity within the broader esports ecosystem. Born in arcades rather than online, the FGC values grassroots competition, community organization, and player-driven events. Unlike most esports, which are heavily controlled by game publishers, the FGC has historically organized its own tournaments, built its own commentary talent, and cultivated its own culture — from "salty runbacks" (immediate rematches after a loss) to the "pop-off" (the celebratory reaction after a clutch victory). While publisher involvement has grown — with Capcom, Bandai Namco, and others now running official circuits — the FGC's grassroots spirit remains its defining characteristic. Local weeklies and regional tournaments continue to serve as the foundation of the community, ensuring that anyone with enough skill can rise to the top.

Structure

How Esports Seasons Work

Understanding the structure of esports competition is key to following the scene. Most esports follow a seasonal format that builds toward a world championship, but the specifics vary significantly by game.

🌐 The Path to Worlds

Regional Leagues → International Events → World Championship

Most major esports follow a three-tier competitive structure. At the base, regional leagues operate throughout the year, split into seasons (often called "splits"). Teams compete in their home region — for example, the LCK (Korea), LPL (China), LEC (Europe), or LCS (North America) in League of Legends.

Between splits, the top teams from each region meet at international events — like the Mid-Season Invitational (MSI) in League of Legends or Masters events in Valorant. These events build rivalries and test regional strength.

At the end of the season, the best teams from every region qualify for the World Championship, the ultimate prize. Qualification is typically based on a combination of Championship Points earned throughout the season and performance in the final regional split.

🛠 Competitive Models

Franchise Model vs. Open Circuit

Esports uses two primary competitive models. The franchise model — used by the LCS, CDL (Call of Duty League), and formerly the Overwatch League — features a fixed number of teams that purchase permanent slots. There is no promotion or relegation. This model provides financial stability but limits the number of participating organizations.

The open circuit model — used by CS2, the FGC, and many other games — allows any team or player to compete in qualifying tournaments and earn their way to the top. This model is more meritocratic and allows for more organic competition, but can be less financially stable for organizations.

Many games use a hybrid approach. Valorant's VCT features partnered leagues (a semi-franchise model) with promotion/relegation through Challengers leagues, while also incorporating open qualifiers at certain stages. League of Legends uses franchised regional leagues but an open international tournament structure.

🇧🇷 Regions & Seeding

Regions, Qualification & Seeding

Esports competition is organized around geographic regions, each with its own competitive ecosystem. In most games, the major regions are Korea, China, Europe, North America, Southeast Asia, and sometimes Latin America, Brazil, Japan, and Oceania.

The number of spots each region receives at a world championship depends on the region's historical performance. Stronger regions (like Korea and China in League of Legends) receive more slots than weaker ones. This system incentivizes regional strength and creates meaningful international rivalries.

Seeding — how teams are placed in groups and brackets at international events — is typically determined by a combination of regional league placement and past international results. Proper seeding aims to prevent the two strongest teams from meeting early in a tournament, building toward the most compelling possible matchups in the later rounds.

📈 Growth & Comparison

Esports vs. Traditional Sports

Esports viewership now exceeds 500 million unique viewers globally, a figure comparable to sports like tennis and baseball in global reach. The 2023 League of Legends World Championship final drew more peak concurrent viewers than many NFL regular-season games, Premier League matches, and NBA playoff games.

However, esports still trails the very largest traditional sporting events. The FIFA World Cup final (over 1.5 billion viewers), the Super Bowl (113M+ US viewers), and the UEFA Champions League final (over 400 million viewers) remain significantly larger in total audience.

Where esports excels is in the 18-34 demographic, where it often outperforms traditional sports in engagement metrics. The esports audience is younger, more digitally native, and highly engaged on platforms like Twitch, YouTube, and bilibili.

Revenue growth has been strong but uneven. Global esports revenue has grown from approximately $500 million in 2016 to over $1.8 billion, though profitability remains a challenge for many organizations. The industry is transitioning from a growth-at-all-costs phase to a more sustainable economic model.

Organized by Riot Games, Worlds brings together the best teams from regional leagues across Korea (LCK), China (LPL), Europe (LEC), North America (LCS), and other regions. The Fortnite World Cup 2019 offered a $30 million prize pool entirely funded by Epic Games, with solo winner Bugha earning $3 million.

The League of Legends World Championship typically offers a prize pool of around $2-3 million, though players also earn substantial salaries from their teams — top LCK and LPL players can earn millions annually from salary alone. CS2 Major Championships offer $1.25 million per Major.

Beyond tournament winnings, top esports professionals earn significant income from team salaries, sponsorships, streaming revenue, and content creation. The highest-earning players can make well over $5 million per year in total compensation. However, earnings are highly concentrated at the top — the vast majority of professional esports players earn modest incomes.

The League of Legends World Championship finals have been held in venues like the Seoul Gocheok Sky Dome, the Accor Arena in Paris, and the Chase Center in San Francisco. The International has been held at venues like the Rogers Arena in Vancouver and the Climate Pledge Arena in Seattle. CS2 Majors are held in large arenas across Europe and beyond. The atmosphere at live esports events combines the energy of a rock concert with the intensity of a championship sporting event. Crowd reactions to clutch plays and unexpected upsets are a major part of the experience.

Qualification for esports world championships varies by game but generally follows a regional-to-international pathway.

In League of Legends, teams compete in regional leagues (LCK, LPL, LEC, LCS, etc.) across Spring and Summer splits. The top teams from each regional league earn slots at the World Championship based on their region's historical performance and Championship Points.

In CS2, teams earn ranking points through Valve-sponsored Regional Major Ranking (RMR) events, with the top teams from each region qualifying for the Major.

In Dota 2, qualification uses a points-based system from the Dota Pro Circuit (DPC), where teams accumulate points through regional leagues and international events throughout the year.

In Valorant, partnered teams compete in international leagues with periodic open qualification through the Challengers system, building toward the Champions tournament at the end of the year.

For fighting games, players earn ranking points through a circuit of tournaments (like the Capcom Pro Tour), with the top-ranked players qualifying for the year-end championship.

Whether esports qualifies as a "real sport" depends on how you define the term, but by most meaningful measures, competitive gaming shares the core attributes of traditional sports: structured competition, professional athletes, rigorous training regimens, organized leagues and tournaments, massive audiences, and significant prize money.

Esports athletes train for 8-12 hours per day, maintain physical fitness routines to support cognitive performance, and compete under intense pressure in front of millions of viewers. The hand-eye coordination, reaction time, strategic thinking, and teamwork required at the highest levels are extraordinary.

Several countries have officially recognized esports as a sport, and competitive gaming was featured as an official medal event at the 2023 Asian Games in Hangzhou, China — a multi-sport event recognized by the International Olympic Committee. The IOC has explored including esports in future Olympic programming.

At ChampionshipsGuide, we rate esports championships using the same tier system as traditional sports, evaluating them on global audience, competitive depth, historical prestige, and cultural significance. The debate around whether esports is a "real sport" is increasingly becoming a semantic question rather than a substantive one — the competition, skill, and passion are undeniable.

The most popular esports games span several genres:

MOBA (Multiplayer Online Battle Arena): League of Legends and Dota 2 are the dominant titles, with League of Legends commanding the largest global esports audience of any game.

Tactical Shooters: Counter-Strike 2 (CS2) and Valorant lead the way. CS2 carries over two decades of competitive heritage, while Valorant has grown rapidly since its 2020 launch.

Battle Royale: Fortnite and PUBG have significant competitive scenes, with Fortnite particularly popular among younger audiences.

Fighting Games: Street Fighter 6, Tekken 8, Guilty Gear Strive, and Super Smash Bros. maintain passionate competitive communities organized around the FGC.

Other notable esports: Rocket League (car soccer), Rainbow Six Siege (tactical FPS), EA Sports FC (sports simulation), Overwatch 2 (hero shooter), and StarCraft II (real-time strategy, historically significant).

The popularity of specific esports titles shifts over time as new games launch and existing games evolve. The esports landscape is more diverse than ever, with dozens of games maintaining active professional competitive scenes.
