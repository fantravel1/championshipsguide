Championships by Format

Not all championships work the same way. Explore the formats that define how champions are crowned.

Format Types

How Championships Are Structured

Every championship uses a format — a set of rules that determines how a winner is decided. Some crown a champion in a single match; others take an entire season. Here are the six fundamental championship formats in world sport.

🏆

Single-Game Finals

One match decides everything. The highest-pressure format in sport — win and you are immortal, lose and it haunts you forever. Used by the Super Bowl, FA Cup Final, and Champions League Final.
🏀

Best-of-Series

Extended series where the first team to win a set number of games takes the title. Rewards consistency, allows comebacks, and produces epic rivalries. Used by the NBA Finals, Stanley Cup, and World Series.
⚡

Knockout Tournaments

Lose once, go home. Single-elimination brackets that produce Cinderella stories, stunning upsets, and the most unpredictable moments in sport. Used by March Madness, the FA Cup, and Wimbledon.
🌐

Group Stage + Knockout

A two-phase format that balances fairness with drama. Teams first survive pool play, then face sudden death in the knockout rounds. Used by the FIFA World Cup, Rugby World Cup, and Champions League.
🏅

Season-Long Races

Points accumulate across an entire season of competition. No single match is decisive, but every result matters. The most complete competitor wins. Used by Formula 1, the Premier League, and La Liga.
⏰

Multi-Day Endurance

Marathon events that span days or weeks, testing physical endurance, mental resilience, and strategic depth. Used by the Tour de France, Grand Slam tennis, The Masters, and the 24 Hours of Le Mans.
Format 1

Single-Game Finals

One match. One winner. No second chances. The single-game final is the purest and most high-pressure championship format in sport.

How It Works

In a single-game final, the entire championship comes down to one decisive match. After weeks, months, or even years of qualifying rounds, league play, and playoff brackets, two teams or competitors meet in a single contest. The winner is the champion. There is no Game 2, no second leg, no do-over. When the final whistle blows, one side lifts the trophy and the other goes home with nothing.

This format is used across a wide range of sports and is arguably the most iconic way to decide a champion. The entire season funnels into one moment, creating a spectacle that transcends the sport itself.

Why It Is So Dramatic

The single-game final produces the highest-pressure single moment in sport. There are no second chances. A team can dominate an entire season, win every match along the way, and then lose everything in 90 minutes — or 60 minutes, or four quarters. This is what makes these events so compelling: the margins are razor-thin, the stakes are absolute, and anything can happen.

A single injury, a controversial refereeing decision, a moment of individual brilliance, or a catastrophic error can swing the outcome. The 2005 Champions League Final, where Liverpool came back from 3-0 down against AC Milan at halftime to win on penalties, is a perfect example of how a single-game final can produce moments that define an entire sport.

The Pressure Factor

Studies have shown that the physiological stress experienced by athletes in single-game finals is significantly higher than in any game of a playoff series. Heart rates, cortisol levels, and self-reported anxiety all peak during winner-take-all scenarios. This pressure is what separates legends from the merely great — the ability to perform at your absolute best when the stakes are highest and there is no safety net.

Notable Examples

American Football

Super Bowl

The biggest single-day sporting event in the United States. Over 110 million domestic viewers watch the AFC and NFC champions meet in one winner-take-all game every February. The Super Bowl is as much a cultural event as a championship — with its legendary halftime show and million-dollar commercials.

Football

FA Cup Final

The oldest football competition in the world, founded in 1871. The FA Cup Final at Wembley Stadium is a single match that decides England's most romantic cup competition. Known for its history of giant-killings, where lower-league clubs topple Premier League titans.

Football

Champions League Final

The pinnacle of European club football. After months of group stages and two-legged knockout ties, the two best clubs in Europe meet in a single-match final at a neutral venue. The Champions League Final is the most-watched annual sporting event in the world.

Australian Football

AFL Grand Final

Australia's biggest domestic sporting event. Held at the Melbourne Cricket Ground before 100,000 fans every September, the AFL Grand Final is a single match between the top two teams in Australian rules football. The atmosphere at the MCG on Grand Final day is one of the most intense in world sport.

Football

Copa del Rey Final

Spain's premier knockout cup competition. The Copa del Rey Final is a single-match showpiece that pits two Spanish clubs against each other in a winner-take-all contest, often featuring the giants of La Liga in dramatic encounters that captivate the football world.

Pros and Cons of the Single-Game Final

Advantages

Maximum drama — every second matters and a single play can change history.

Cultural event — single-game finals become national or global spectacles that transcend sport (Super Bowl, Champions League Final).

Accessibility — casual fans can watch one game and experience the full championship moment.

Neutral venue spectacle — many finals are held at iconic neutral stadiums, creating a unique event atmosphere.

Disadvantages

Randomness — the better team does not always win in a single game; injuries, refereeing errors, and bad luck can be decisive.

Anticlimactic potential — a blowout final (common in the Super Bowl) can deflate the spectacle.

No recovery from slow starts — unlike a series, there is no chance to adjust tactics or bounce back from an early deficit.

Travel and ticket scarcity — with only one game, ticket demand massively outstrips supply.

Format 2

Best-of-Series

Extended series where the first team to win a set number of games claims the title. The format rewards consistency, depth, and resilience.

How It Works

In a best-of-series format, two teams play a predetermined number of games, and the first team to win a majority of those games wins the championship. The most common formats are best-of-seven (first to four wins) and best-of-five (first to three wins). Home-court advantage typically alternates in a set pattern — for example, in a best-of-seven, the team with the better regular-season record might host Games 1, 2, 5, and 7 (the 2-2-1-1-1 format).

The series ends as soon as one team reaches the required number of wins. A team that sweeps (wins every game) finishes in the minimum number of games, while a series that goes to the maximum (Game 7 in a best-of-seven) produces some of the most intense and dramatic moments in all of sport.

Why This Format Works

Rewards consistency — a team cannot win a best-of-seven series by getting lucky once. They must perform at a high level across multiple games, which means the truly better team almost always prevails. This reduces the randomness that can define single-game finals.

Reduces randomness — over seven games, individual performances, officiating decisions, and random bounces tend to even out. The series reveals which team is genuinely superior, not just which team had a better night.

Dramatic comebacks are possible — the best-of-series format allows for one of the most thrilling narratives in sport: the comeback from a series deficit. The Cleveland Cavaliers' comeback from 3-1 down against the 73-win Golden State Warriors in the 2016 NBA Finals is widely considered one of the greatest achievements in sports history. In a single-game final, such a comeback narrative is simply impossible.

Notable Examples

Basketball

NBA Finals

A best-of-seven series between the Eastern and Western Conference champions. The NBA Finals have produced some of the most legendary individual performances in sport — from Michael Jordan's flu game to LeBron James's chase-down block. The series format allows stars to build a narrative across multiple games.

Ice Hockey

Stanley Cup

The NHL's championship is decided through a best-of-seven Stanley Cup Final, which itself comes after three rounds of best-of-seven playoff series. Winning the Stanley Cup requires winning 16 games across four rounds — the most grueling championship gauntlet in North American professional sport.

Baseball

World Series

The championship of Major League Baseball, contested as a best-of-seven series since 1905. The World Series is called the "Fall Classic" and carries more than a century of history, drama, and tradition. Walk-off home runs in Game 7 of the World Series are among the most iconic moments in American sports.

Basketball

WNBA Finals

The championship series of the world's premier women's professional basketball league. The WNBA Finals have grown dramatically in profile and viewership, with the best-of-five format (expanded to best-of-seven in recent years) producing compelling matchups and rising stars.

Aggregate Scoring in Football

European and South American football use a variant of the series format called aggregate scoring (or two-legged ties). Rather than playing a best-of-seven series, two teams play one match at each team's home ground (a "home leg" and an "away leg"). The total goals across both legs determine the winner.

This format is used in the Champions League knockout rounds, the Copa Libertadores, and many other continental and domestic cup competitions. Aggregate scoring creates a unique strategic dynamic: a team that loses the first leg 2-1 away from home knows exactly what they need to do in the second leg at home. This leads to dramatic second legs where teams push forward desperately to overturn deficits.

If the aggregate score is level after both legs, the match goes to extra time and then a penalty shootout in the second leg. UEFA abolished the away goals rule in 2021 — previously, if the aggregate was tied, the team with more goals scored away from home would advance. The removal of this rule has led to even more extra-time drama in recent seasons.

Historic Aggregate Comebacks

Some of the greatest moments in football history have come from aggregate comebacks. In the 2018-19 Champions League, Liverpool overturned a 3-0 first-leg deficit against Barcelona to win 4-0 at Anfield and advance 4-3 on aggregate. On the same night in the other semifinal, Tottenham overcame a 3-0 aggregate deficit against Ajax with a dramatic second-half hat trick from Lucas Moura. These moments are only possible because of the two-legged aggregate format.

Format 3

Knockout Tournaments

Lose once, go home. Single-elimination brackets produce the most unpredictable and emotionally intense championships in sport.

How It Works

A knockout tournament (also called single elimination) is structured as a bracket where competitors face off in head-to-head matches. The loser of each match is immediately eliminated from the tournament, and the winner advances to the next round. The bracket progressively narrows — from 64 teams to 32, to 16, to 8, to 4 (the semifinals), to 2 (the final) — until only one competitor remains: the champion.

Seeding is typically used to ensure that the strongest competitors are placed on opposite sides of the bracket, theoretically meeting only in the later rounds. However, the beauty of the knockout format is that seeding means nothing once the games begin — any team can beat any other team on any given day.

Why This Format Captivates

Cinderella stories — knockout tournaments are the breeding ground for sport's greatest fairy tales. When a 16th seed beats a 1st seed, when a tiny school from a tiny town makes the Final Four, when a fourth-division football club reaches the FA Cup semifinals — these are the stories that make sport transcendent. The knockout format makes them possible because every underdog only needs to win one game at a time.

Upsets and madness — the term "March Madness" exists for a reason. When there is no margin for error, when a single bad performance means elimination, chaos ensues. Superior teams lose to inferior ones far more often in knockout formats than in any other. This unpredictability is what makes these tournaments so addictive.

Win-or-go-home intensity — every single match in a knockout tournament carries elimination stakes. This creates a level of intensity and emotional investment that is almost impossible to replicate in other formats. Players perform at superhuman levels because they know there is no tomorrow.

Notable Examples

Basketball

NCAA March Madness

The most dramatic tournament in American sports. Sixty-eight college basketball teams compete in a single-elimination bracket over three weeks, producing more upsets, buzzer-beaters, and Cinderella stories than any other event. Over 100 million Americans fill out brackets annually, making it a national obsession.

Football

FA Cup

The oldest football competition in the world, founded in 1871. The FA Cup is a single-elimination knockout tournament that includes clubs from every level of English football. Its "magic" comes from the possibility that a team from the fifth tier can draw a Premier League giant and, in one match, pull off the impossible.

Tennis

Wimbledon

The most prestigious tennis tournament in the world operates as a 128-player knockout bracket. Over two weeks, players must win seven consecutive matches to claim the title. One bad day and you are out. The knockout format means that even the greatest players in history — Federer, Nadal, Djokovic — have suffered shocking early exits.

Football

World Cup Knockout Rounds

After the group stage, the FIFA World Cup transitions to a single-elimination knockout format — the Round of 16, quarterfinals, semifinals, and the final. These rounds produce some of the most memorable moments in sporting history: penalty shootout heartbreaks, last-minute winners, and the sheer agony and ecstasy of win-or-go-home football.
Format 4

Group Stage + Knockout

The two-phase format that balances fairness with drama. Teams earn the right to compete in the knockout rounds by surviving the group stage first.

How It Works

The group-stage-plus-knockout format is a two-phase championship structure. In the first phase (the group stage), teams are divided into groups (also called pools) and play a round-robin within their group. Points are awarded for wins (typically 3 points) and draws (1 point), with zero for losses. At the end of the group stage, the top teams from each group advance to the knockout phase.

In the second phase (the knockout rounds), the format switches to single elimination. Teams that advanced from the group stage face off in a bracket, with losers eliminated immediately. This continues through the Round of 16, quarterfinals, semifinals, and the final.

This format is considered the gold standard for major international tournaments because it balances two competing priorities: giving every team a fair chance to demonstrate their quality (group stage), while still delivering the high-stakes drama of win-or-go-home matches (knockout rounds).

Why It Balances Fairness With Drama

The group stage ensures that no team is eliminated based on a single bad performance. Unlike a pure knockout tournament where a slow start means instant elimination, the group stage gives teams three (or more) matches to prove themselves. A team that loses its opening match can still qualify by winning its remaining games. This means the strongest teams almost always advance, which gives the knockout rounds a higher baseline quality.

Once the knockout phase begins, all the fairness safeguards disappear and the raw drama of single-elimination takes over. This combination produces tournaments that are both credible (the best teams tend to advance) and dramatic (anything can happen once the knockouts start).

Notable Examples

Football

FIFA World Cup

The most-watched sporting event on the planet uses a group-stage-plus-knockout format. Thirty-two teams (expanding to 48 in 2026) are divided into groups of four. The top two from each group advance to a single-elimination knockout bracket that culminates in the World Cup Final.

Rugby

Rugby World Cup

Twenty teams compete in four pools of five during the group stage. The top two teams from each pool advance to the knockout quarterfinals. The Rugby World Cup's group stage often produces tense final-day calculations, where multiple teams' fates hang on point differentials and bonus points.

Football

Champions League

Since 2024-25, the Champions League features a reformed league phase where 36 teams each play eight matches against different opponents. The top eight advance directly to the Round of 16, while teams ranked 9th-24th enter a two-legged knockout playoff. The competition then proceeds through quarterfinals, semifinals, and a single-match final.

Cricket

Cricket World Cup

The ICC Cricket World Cup has used several variants of the group-stage-plus-knockout format. Recent editions have featured a round-robin group stage followed by semifinals and a final. The 2023 tournament in India used a 10-team round-robin where each team played every other team once, with the top four advancing to the semifinals.

How Group Stage Tiebreakers Work

When two or more teams finish the group stage with the same number of points, tiebreakers are used to determine who advances. The exact tiebreaker rules vary by competition, but the most common system is:

  1. Goal difference (or point differential) — the difference between goals scored and goals conceded across all group matches. A team that wins 3-0 and loses 0-1 has a goal difference of +2.
  2. Goals scored — if goal difference is equal, the team that scored more total goals advances. This encourages attacking play even in dead-rubber matches.
  3. Head-to-head record — the result of the match between the tied teams. Some competitions (like the European Championship) use head-to-head as the first tiebreaker rather than overall goal difference.
  4. Fair play record — disciplinary record (yellow and red cards) is sometimes used as a final tiebreaker. At the 2018 FIFA World Cup, Japan advanced over Senegal based on fair play points — the first time this tiebreaker was ever used at a World Cup.
  5. Drawing of lots — in the extremely unlikely event that all other tiebreakers are equal, a random draw may be used. This has never been required at a FIFA World Cup.

The "Group of Death"

A "Group of Death" is an informal term for a group stage draw where multiple strong teams are placed in the same group, meaning at least one highly favored team will be eliminated before the knockout rounds. The 2014 FIFA World Cup Group D (Italy, Uruguay, England, Costa Rica) is a classic example — three former World Cup winners in the same group, with only two spots available. Costa Rica topped the group, England finished last. The group stage's random draw makes these scenarios inevitable and adds to the drama of the format.

Format 5

Season-Long Championships

Points accumulate over months of competition. No single match decides the title — every race, every match, every weekend matters. The most complete competitor wins.

How Points Accumulate Over a Season

In a season-long championship, competitors earn points (or accumulate standings) across an extended calendar of events. In football leagues, teams play every other team in the division twice (home and away), earning 3 points for a win, 1 for a draw, and 0 for a loss. The team with the most points at the end of the season is the champion. There is no playoff, no final — just the relentless grind of a full season.

In motorsport, drivers earn points at each Grand Prix based on their finishing position. The driver with the most cumulative points at the end of the season is the World Champion. Formula 1 awards 25 points for a race win, 18 for second place, 15 for third, and so on down to 1 point for tenth place. With over 20 races per season, consistency across the entire calendar is essential.

In cycling's Grand Tours, the overall classification (the "General Classification" or GC) is determined by cumulative time across all stages. The rider with the lowest total time across all 21 stages wins — meaning every second lost on a mountain stage or in a time trial accumulates and matters.

Why This Format Rewards the Most Complete Competitor

Season-long championships are the ultimate test of consistency, depth, and endurance. A team or competitor cannot win a season-long title by being brilliant for one week and mediocre the rest. They must perform at a high level week after week, month after month, through injuries, bad form, travel, fatigue, and adversity.

This format also rewards squad depth in team sports. In the Premier League, a team with a deep, talented squad can rotate players and maintain performance across 38 matches, while a team that relies on a few star players will inevitably drop points due to fatigue or injury. The same principle applies in Formula 1, where the reliability and performance of the car across an entire season is as important as any single race result.

Notable Examples

Motorsport

Formula 1 World Championship

The pinnacle of single-seater motor racing awards its World Championship based on points accumulated across 20+ Grands Prix per season, spanning five continents from March to December. The Drivers' Championship and Constructors' Championship are both decided by season-long points tallies.

Football

Premier League

The most-watched football league in the world decides its champion based on 38 matches played across nine months. The Premier League has produced some of the most dramatic title races in sport — including the 2011-12 season, when Manchester City's Sergio Agüero scored in the 94th minute of the final game to clinch the title.

Football

La Liga

Spain's top football division determines its champion through a 38-match season, with 20 teams competing from August to May. Dominated by Barcelona and Real Madrid for decades, La Liga's season-long format has historically rewarded the extraordinary consistency of these two clubs across an entire campaign.

Football

Serie A

Italy's premier football league follows the same season-long format, with 20 teams playing 38 matches each. Serie A's tactical complexity makes it a league where consistency and defensive solidity are especially rewarded over the course of a long season.

Cycling

Tour de France

While the Tour de France is technically a multi-day endurance event (covered in the next section), its General Classification is a season-within-a-season: cumulative time across 21 stages over three weeks determines the champion. Every second counts across mountain stages, time trials, and flat stages.

Last-Day Drama

Season-long championships produce their own unique brand of drama: the final-day title decider. When two teams or drivers arrive at the last round separated by a single point or goal difference, the accumulated tension of an entire season explodes into one final afternoon. The 2011-12 Premier League season (Agüero's last-gasp goal), the 2021 F1 season (Hamilton vs. Verstappen decided on the last lap of the last race), and the 2013-14 La Liga title race (Atlético Madrid winning the title at Camp Nou on the final day) are all examples of season-long formats producing moments of extraordinary, built-up drama that no other format can replicate.

Format 6

Multi-Day Endurance

Marathon events that span days or weeks, testing physical endurance, mental resilience, strategic adaptation, and sheer staying power.

What Makes These Events Unique

Multi-day endurance championships are unlike any other format in sport. They do not end in a single day, a single match, or even a single week. These are marathon events where competitors must sustain peak performance across days, weeks, or multiple rounds of competition. The champion is not the person who has the best single day — it is the person who is the best across all of them.

These events test a dimension of athletic ability that shorter formats cannot: the ability to recover, adapt, and persevere. A cyclist in the Tour de France must climb mountains on day 15 with legs that have already ridden 2,000 kilometers. A tennis player at Wimbledon must win seven matches over two weeks, often playing five-set marathons that last four or five hours. A golfer at The Masters must maintain concentration and precision across 72 holes over four days. The cumulative physical and mental toll is immense.

Multi-day events also reward strategic thinking. In the Tour de France, teams decide when to attack and when to conserve energy across 21 stages. In a Grand Slam tennis tournament, a player might deliberately play a shorter, less taxing style in early rounds to conserve energy for the later rounds when the opposition gets tougher.

Notable Examples

Cycling

Tour de France

The ultimate endurance event in sport. Twenty-one stages over 23 days, covering approximately 3,500 kilometers of French roads, alpine mountain passes, time trials, and sprint stages. Riders burn up to 8,000 calories per day and push their bodies to the absolute limit across three weeks of racing in the July heat.

Tennis

Grand Slam Tennis

Each of the four Grand Slams (Australian Open, French Open, Wimbledon, US Open) is a two-week, 128-player knockout tournament. Players must win seven matches across 14 days, with men playing best-of-five sets in every round. The physical toll of a deep Grand Slam run — especially on the slow clay of Roland-Garros — is immense.

Golf

The Masters

Golf's most exclusive tournament is played over four rounds (72 holes) across four days at Augusta National Golf Club in April. The Masters tests every aspect of a golfer's game across one of the most challenging and beautiful courses in the world. A cut after 36 holes eliminates the bottom half of the field.

Motorsport

24 Hours of Le Mans

The world's oldest active endurance race, held since 1923. Teams of three drivers race prototype and GT cars for 24 continuous hours around the Circuit de la Sarthe in France. The race covers over 5,000 kilometers and demands speed, strategy, mechanical reliability, and the ability to race through the night.

Darts

World Darts Championship

The PDC World Darts Championship spans over two weeks from mid-December to early January. Ninety-six players compete in a single-elimination bracket, with matches increasing in length from best-of-five sets in early rounds to a best-of-thirteen sets final. The tournament's marathon format, combined with the intense atmosphere at Alexandra Palace, creates one of the most unique endurance tests in sport.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Clear answers to common questions about how championships are structured and why formats matter.

A playoff series (such as a best-of-seven) requires a team to win multiple games to claim the championship, whereas a single-match final decides everything in one game. Series formats like the NBA Finals or Stanley Cup reward consistency and depth over multiple games, giving the better team more opportunities to prove themselves and reducing the impact of a single off night.

Single-match finals like the Super Bowl or FA Cup Final create maximum drama because there is no tomorrow — one bad quarter or one mistake can end a season. Series formats allow comebacks from deficits (like the Cleveland Cavaliers' historic 3-1 comeback in the 2016 NBA Finals), while single-game finals are defined by their do-or-die intensity.

Both formats have passionate defenders. The choice of format often reflects the culture and tradition of the sport — American sports leagues tend to favor playoff series, while European football and international tournaments tend to favor single-match finals or knockout formats.

Aggregate scoring is a two-legged format used in European and South American football competitions, most notably in the Champions League knockout rounds and Copa Libertadores. Each team plays one match at home and one away, and the total (aggregate) goals across both matches determine the winner.

For example, if Team A wins the first leg 2-1 at home and Team B wins the second leg 1-0 at their home, the aggregate score is 2-2. In this case, extra time is played in the second leg, followed by a penalty shootout if needed. UEFA abolished the away goals rule in 2021 — previously, if the aggregate was tied, the team with more goals scored away from home would advance.

Aggregate scoring adds a unique strategic dimension because teams must balance attacking intent with defensive caution across two matches. It has produced some of football's greatest moments, including Liverpool's miraculous 4-0 second-leg win against Barcelona in the 2018-19 Champions League semifinals.

A knockout tournament (also called single elimination) is a format where a team or player is eliminated after a single loss. The field is progressively halved each round — from 64 to 32 to 16 to 8 to 4 to 2 — until only one competitor remains.

This format is used in some of the most dramatic championships in sport, including NCAA March Madness (68 teams), the FA Cup in English football, Wimbledon and the other tennis Grand Slams (128 players), and the knockout rounds of the FIFA World Cup.

Knockout tournaments are beloved for their unpredictability, Cinderella stories (where underdogs make deep runs), and the sheer tension of win-or-go-home stakes. The downside is that a single bad performance can end the run of a superior team, which is why some competitions combine a group stage with knockout rounds to balance fairness and excitement.

A group stage (also called pool play or the group phase) is the first phase of a multi-stage tournament where teams are divided into small groups and play a round-robin within their group. Each team plays every other team in the group once (or sometimes twice), earning points for wins and draws. At the end of the group stage, the top teams from each group advance to the knockout rounds.

The FIFA World Cup uses groups of four teams, with the top two from each group advancing. The Champions League (since 2024-25) uses a league phase where 36 teams each play eight matches, with the top eight advancing directly and teams ranked 9th-24th entering a playoff round.

Group stages ensure that teams play multiple matches before elimination, reducing the chance that a single upset ends a strong team's tournament. Tiebreakers in group stages typically consider goal difference, goals scored, head-to-head record, and fair play (disciplinary record).

This is subjective, but many fans and experts consider the single-game final to be the most dramatic championship format. Events like the Super Bowl, FA Cup Final, and Champions League Final compress an entire season's worth of effort into one decisive match with no second chances — producing moments of extraordinary tension, heartbreak, and euphoria.

However, knockout tournaments like NCAA March Madness generate sustained drama over weeks, with constant upsets and Cinderella stories. Best-of-seven series can also produce incredible drama when they go to a decisive Game 7, such as the 2016 NBA Finals or the 2011 Stanley Cup Final.

Season-long championships like Formula 1 or the Premier League create a different kind of drama — a slow-burn tension that builds over months, where a single point or a last-day result can decide a title (like Agüero's last-gasp goal for Manchester City in 2012 or Verstappen vs. Hamilton in 2021).

Ultimately, each format produces its own unique brand of drama, which is part of what makes the world of championships so compelling.
