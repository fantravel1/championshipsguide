City Profile

London

The undisputed capital of world sport. No city on Earth hosts more elite championships across more sports than London.

Championships Hosted in London

London's championship calendar is unrivaled in both depth and variety. The city is home to Wimbledon, the oldest and most prestigious tennis tournament in the world, held at the All England Club since 1877. Every June and July, the grass courts of SW19 become the center of the tennis universe, with the men's and women's singles finals on Centre Court ranking among the most-watched sporting events of the year.

At Wembley Stadium, London hosts the FA Cup Final every May — the climax of the world's oldest football knockout competition, founded in 1871. Wembley has also hosted Champions League finals (most recently in 2024) and serves as the home of the England national football team. The stadium's 90,000-seat capacity makes it one of the largest and most atmospheric venues in world sport.

London is a major rugby union destination, with Twickenham Stadium hosting England's Six Nations matches every spring — one of the oldest and most intense international rugby competitions. The NFL London Games have become an annual fixture at Tottenham Hotspur Stadium, bringing American football to an increasingly enthusiastic British audience.

Other major championships hosted in London include the London Marathon (one of the World Marathon Majors, held every April), The Boat Race between Oxford and Cambridge (rowed on the Thames since 1829), and major cricket test matches and one-day internationals at Lord's Cricket Ground — "the home of cricket." London also hosted the 2012 Olympic Games, widely regarded as one of the most successful in history, and the 2017 World Athletics Championships at the London Stadium.

Best Months to Visit London for Sport

April brings the London Marathon — one of the six World Marathon Majors — and the start of the cricket season. May is dominated by the FA Cup Final at Wembley, the climax of English football's most romantic cup competition. June and July are the peak months, with Wimbledon running for two glorious weeks and rugby test matches filling Twickenham. February and March offer Six Nations rugby weekends, when London becomes a sea of international fans. October and November bring NFL London Games, with multiple American football matches played in the capital.

Travel Tips for London

London's public transport network — the Tube, Overground, and bus system — makes reaching every major venue straightforward. Wimbledon is served by the District Line and South Western Railway. Wembley has its own Tube station (Wembley Park on the Metropolitan and Jubilee lines). Twickenham is best reached by train from Waterloo. For accommodation, book early during Wimbledon fortnight and FA Cup Final weekend — central London hotels fill up fast. Budget-conscious fans should consider staying in well-connected neighborhoods like Stratford (near the Olympic Park), Kings Cross, or Southwark. London is an expensive city, but many championship experiences — such as standing on the Putney Bridge to watch The Boat Race — are completely free.

City Profile

New York

The city that never sleeps is also the city that never stops competing. New York's sports scene is unmatched in North America.

Championships Hosted in New York

New York is home to the US Open, the final tennis Grand Slam of the calendar year, held every August and September at the USTA Billie Jean King National Tennis Center in Flushing Meadows, Queens. Arthur Ashe Stadium, with its 23,771-seat capacity, is the largest tennis-specific stadium in the world. The US Open's electric night sessions — under the lights in New York — produce an atmosphere unlike anything else in tennis.

MetLife Stadium in East Rutherford, New Jersey (the NYC metropolitan area) is an 82,500-seat colossus that hosts both the New York Giants and New York Jets of the NFL. It has hosted the Super Bowl (Super Bowl XLVIII in 2014) and will host matches during the 2026 FIFA World Cup, including the tournament final — bringing the biggest event in world sport to the Big Apple.

Madison Square Garden — universally known as "The World's Most Famous Arena" — hosts the New York Knicks (NBA) and New York Rangers (NHL). When the Knicks or Rangers make deep playoff runs, MSG becomes one of the most intense atmospheres in all of professional sport. The arena also hosts world championship boxing and UFC title fights, plus marquee college basketball events.

The New York City Marathon, held every November, is the world's largest marathon with over 50,000 runners and two million spectators lining the course through all five boroughs. Yankee Stadium in the Bronx hosts the New York Yankees (MLB), one of the most storied franchises in professional sport, while Barclays Center in Brooklyn hosts Brooklyn Nets (NBA) games and major boxing events.

Best Months to Visit New York for Sport

August and September are the peak months for New York sports fans, with the US Open dominating the late-summer calendar. The tournament's night sessions under the Arthur Ashe Stadium lights are a quintessential New York experience. February brings the Super Bowl (when the region hosts) and the build-up to the NFL postseason. October is ideal for baseball fans hoping to catch the Yankees in the MLB postseason, and the NBA and NHL seasons are in full swing. November is marathon month, with the TCS New York City Marathon turning the city into a global running festival. April through June is NBA and NHL playoff season — and if the Knicks or Rangers are in contention, there is no atmosphere in American sport quite like a playoff game at Madison Square Garden.

Travel Tips for New York

New York's subway system (the MTA) provides access to most major venues. The 7 train runs directly to the US Open at Mets-Willets Point station, and the subway connects to Barclays Center (Atlantic Avenue) and is a short ride from Madison Square Garden (Penn Station). MetLife Stadium requires the NJ Transit bus or train from Penn Station. For accommodation, Midtown Manhattan is the most convenient base for most sporting events, but neighborhoods like Long Island City (Queens) and Downtown Brooklyn offer better value with excellent subway connections. New York is expensive year-round, but particularly during US Open fortnight and marathon weekend. Book accommodation and flights at least three to four months in advance for these events.

City Profile

Paris

The City of Light is also the City of Champions. Paris pairs sporting excellence with cultural grandeur like nowhere else on Earth.

Championships Hosted in Paris

Paris is the home of Roland-Garros, the French Open — the world's premier clay-court tennis tournament and one of the four Grand Slams. Held every May and June, Roland-Garros is famous for its red clay courts, its intimate Philippe-Chatrier court, and the grueling physical demands it places on players. The French Open is widely considered the most physically challenging Grand Slam, and winning it on clay is one of the greatest achievements in tennis.

Every July, Paris plays host to the most iconic finish in world cycling: the final stage of the Tour de France on the Champs-Elysées. Millions of fans line the famous avenue to watch the peloton sprint down the most beautiful boulevard in the world, celebrating the conclusion of three weeks and 3,500 kilometers of racing through the French countryside and mountains.

Paris hosted the 2024 Olympic and Paralympic Games, staging events at iconic locations including the Stade de France, the Eiffel Tower (beach volleyball), and the Palace of Versailles (equestrian). The Stade de France in Saint-Denis (81,338 capacity) is the primary venue for French national team football and rugby matches, hosting Six Nations and Ligue 1 fixtures. It also served as the main venue for the 2023 Rugby World Cup, including the final.

Parc des Princes is the home of Paris Saint-Germain (PSG) and the venue for the club's Champions League home matches, which regularly feature some of the biggest names in world football. Paris has hosted multiple Champions League finals at the Stade de France. The city also hosts regular Ligue 1 action, the top division of French football, as well as major boxing events and the Paris Masters (ATP 1000 tennis) at the Accor Arena (formerly Bercy).

Best Months to Visit Paris for Sport

May and June are the golden months for sport in Paris, with the French Open at Roland-Garros filling two weeks with world-class tennis. The clay-court season in Paris is also complemented by late-season Champions League action for PSG. July brings the spectacular Tour de France finish on the Champs-Elysées — an absolutely unmissable experience that is free to watch from the roadside. February and March offer Six Nations rugby at the Stade de France, where France's home matches create a passionate, festive atmosphere. October and November bring the Paris Masters indoor tennis tournament at Bercy, one of the biggest ATP events of the season and a warm-up for the year-end finals.

Travel Tips for Paris

Paris has one of the world's best metro systems. The Stade de France is accessible via RER Line B or D (Saint-Denis station). Roland-Garros is reached via Metro Line 9 or 10 (Porte d'Auteuil or Michel-Ange Auteuil). Parc des Princes is near Porte de Saint-Cloud on Metro Line 9. For accommodation during the French Open, the 16th arrondissement (near Roland-Garros) is ideal but expensive — consider the 14th or 15th for better value with easy metro access. For the Tour de France finish, arrive early on the Champs-Elysées to secure a good viewing spot along the barriers. Paris is a year-round destination, so combining a championship visit with the city's museums, restaurants, and culture is effortless. Be aware that accommodation prices rise significantly during Roland-Garros fortnight and any major rugby weekends.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about attending championships in the world's greatest sporting cities.

London hosts more world-class championships than almost any other city on Earth. Annual events include Wimbledon (the oldest and most prestigious tennis Grand Slam, held at the All England Club every June-July), the FA Cup Final (at Wembley Stadium in May), the Six Nations rugby matches at Twickenham, the London Marathon (April), and The Boat Race between Oxford and Cambridge on the Thames (March-April).

London also regularly hosts NFL International Series games at Tottenham Hotspur Stadium. The city has hosted the Olympic Games three times (1908, 1948, 2012), the Champions League final at Wembley, and the 2017 World Athletics Championships at the London Stadium. Key venues include Wembley Stadium (90,000 capacity), the All England Club (Wimbledon), Twickenham Stadium (82,000), The O2 Arena, and Lord's Cricket Ground.

New York City and its metropolitan area host a remarkable concentration of championship events. The US Open tennis Grand Slam takes place at the USTA Billie Jean King National Tennis Center in Flushing Meadows every August-September. MetLife Stadium hosts NFL games for both the New York Giants and Jets, and has hosted the Super Bowl (XLVIII in 2014). It will also host 2026 FIFA World Cup matches, including the final.

Madison Square Garden — "The World's Most Famous Arena" — hosts New York Knicks (NBA) and New York Rangers (NHL) games, including potential playoff and finals matchups, as well as world championship boxing and UFC title fights. The NYC Marathon in November is the world's largest marathon with over 50,000 runners. Yankee Stadium and Barclays Center round out one of the most venue-rich metropolitan areas in world sport.

Paris is one of the world's premier championship destinations. The French Open (Roland-Garros) is held every May-June and is the most prestigious clay-court tennis tournament in the world — one of the four Grand Slams. The Tour de France traditionally finishes on the Champs-Elysées in Paris every July.

Paris hosted the 2024 Olympic and Paralympic Games and the 2023 Rugby World Cup at the Stade de France. The Stade de France (81,338 capacity) hosts French national team football and rugby matches, including the Six Nations. Parc des Princes is home to Paris Saint-Germain in Ligue 1 and Champions League action. Paris has hosted multiple Champions League finals. The Paris Masters (ATP 1000 tennis) is held at the Accor Arena every October-November.

The best championship to travel to depends on your sport preference, budget, and the kind of experience you want. For a bucket-list atmosphere in a world-class city, Wimbledon in London (June-July) and the French Open in Paris (May-June) combine elite sport with iconic destinations.

For an American sports spectacle, the Super Bowl (February) and NBA Finals (June) deliver unmatched energy. For accessibility and value, the Tour de France is free to watch along the route, and the Six Nations rugby offers fantastic matchday atmospheres in cities like London, Paris, Dublin, and Edinburgh.

If you want once-in-a-lifetime rarity, check whether the FIFA World Cup or Olympic Games are scheduled — these quadrennial events are worth planning years in advance. Planning travel for a championship final requires advance preparation — start 6-12 months ahead for major events like the World Cup, Olympics, or Wimbledon. Key steps include:

1. Check the Finals Calendar for exact dates and host cities.

2. Research ticket availability — some events use ballot/lottery systems (Wimbledon, Olympics) while others sell through official channels or authorized resellers.

3. Book accommodation early — hotels near major finals sell out fast and prices surge during championship weeks.

4. Plan transportation — including flights, airport transfers, and local transit to the venue.

5. Check visa requirements for international events in other countries.

6. Arrive early — consider arriving a few days before to explore the host city and soak in the pre-event atmosphere. Many championship cities offer fan zones, public screenings, and cultural events in the days leading up to the final.
