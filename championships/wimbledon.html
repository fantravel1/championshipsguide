Wimbledon

The oldest, most prestigious tennis tournament in the world. Grass courts, all-white dress code, strawberries and cream — Wimbledon is where tradition meets sporting excellence.

Why It's Tier 2 — Elite Global

The most prestigious Grand Slam. The most iconic venue in tennis. 147+ years of history.

The Most Prestigious Grand Slam

While all four Grand Slams are equal in ranking points, Wimbledon occupies a unique position in tennis's hierarchy. It's the oldest (1877), the only one on grass, and carries a weight of tradition and ceremony that the other Slams — great as they are — simply cannot match.

Tradition Like No Other

The all-white dress code, the Royal Box, the ivy on Centre Court, the strawberries and cream, The Queue — Wimbledon is a living museum of sporting tradition. It has resisted commercial modernization while remaining at the cutting edge of tennis.

Centre Court

Centre Court at Wimbledon is arguably the most famous court in all of sport. With its retractable roof (added in 2009), its manicured grass, and its 14,979 seats, it has witnessed every great moment in tennis history. Playing on Centre Court is every tennis player's dream.

Global Audience

Wimbledon is broadcast in over 200 territories worldwide. The men's final draws hundreds of millions of viewers globally. When Andy Murray won in 2013 — the first British men's champion in 77 years — it was watched by 17.3 million in the UK alone.

History of Wimbledon

From a Victorian garden party to the world's most prestigious tennis event.

1877–1945: The Early Years

The first Wimbledon Championships took place in 1877, with 22 men competing in a gentlemen's singles event at the All England Croquet and Lawn Tennis Club. Spencer Gore won the inaugural title. Women's singles was added in 1884. In the early years, the defending champion received a bye to the final (the "Challenge Round") — a system abolished in 1922. The tournament moved to its current Church Road location in 1922, where King George V opened the new grounds. Wimbledon was interrupted by both World Wars, with Centre Court suffering bomb damage in 1940.

1968–1980: The Open Era Begins

1968 marked the dawn of the Open Era, when professionals were finally allowed to compete alongside amateurs. Rod Laver won the first Open Era Wimbledon. The 1970s brought iconic rivalries: Billie Jean King won 6 titles and championed gender equality, while Björn Borg and John McEnroe created tennis's greatest rivalry. Borg won 5 consecutive titles (1976-1980), and the 1980 final against McEnroe — featuring a 34-point fourth-set tiebreak — is considered one of the greatest matches in history.

1980s–1990s: Power & Grace

Martina Navratilova dominated women's tennis with a record 9 Wimbledon singles titles (1978-79, 1982-87, 1990). Boris Becker became the youngest men's champion at 17 in 1985. Stefan Edberg brought elegance. Then came Pete Sampras, who won 7 titles between 1993 and 2000 — his serve-and-volley game perfectly suited to grass. Steffi Graf matched the men's excellence with 7 titles of her own. The Williams sisters emerged late in the decade, with Venus winning in 2000.

2003–2019: The Big Three Era

Roger Federer's Wimbledon reign defined an era — 8 titles including 5 consecutive from 2003-2007. His grass-court artistry was tennis perfection. Rafael Nadal interrupted his dominance in the epic 2008 final, a match widely considered the greatest ever played: 5 sets as darkness fell over Centre Court, with Nadal winning 9-7 in the fifth. Novak Djokovic won 7 titles, including an extraordinary 2019 final where he saved two match points against Federer and won a fifth-set tiebreak. Serena Williams won 7 more titles, cementing her status as one of the greatest athletes in history.

2020s: The New Generation

Wimbledon was cancelled in 2020 for the first time since World War II due to COVID-19. Djokovic won in 2021 and 2022, equalling Sampras's 7 titles. Then came Carlos Alcaraz — the 20-year-old Spaniard who dethroned Djokovic in a five-set 2023 final, announcing a generational shift. Alcaraz defended successfully in 2024, while Barbora Krejčíková took the women's title. A new era of Wimbledon champions is being written.

The Grand Slam Calendar

Where Wimbledon fits in tennis's four major championships.

Australian Open

January — Melbourne, Australia. Hard court. "The Happy Slam." Known for extreme heat and late-night matches. Djokovic's kingdom (10 titles).

French Open (Roland-Garros)

May–June — Paris, France. Clay court. The ultimate test of endurance and patience. Nadal's domain (14 titles — the most at any single Grand Slam).

Wimbledon

June–July — London, England. Grass court. The oldest and most prestigious. Tradition, elegance, and the fastest surface. Federer's spiritual home (8 titles).

US Open

August–September — New York City, USA. Hard court. The loudest, most electric Grand Slam. Night sessions under the lights at Arthur Ashe Stadium (23,771 seats — the largest tennis venue in the world).

Iconic Wimbledon Moments

1980 — Borg vs McEnroe: The Tiebreak

The 1980 men's final featured a legendary fourth-set tiebreak: McEnroe saved 5 championship points, eventually winning it 18-16. But Borg, with ice in his veins, won the fifth set 8-6 to claim his 5th consecutive Wimbledon title. The contrast — Borg's Scandinavian cool vs McEnroe's American fire — defined an era.

2008 — Nadal vs Federer: The Greatest Match

Widely considered the greatest tennis match ever played. Rafael Nadal defeated Roger Federer in 5 sets (6-4, 6-4, 6-7, 6-7, 9-7) as darkness descended on Centre Court. Rain delays, momentum swings, Federer saving two championship points — it had everything. When Nadal fell to his knees in near-darkness at 9:15pm, it marked the end of Federer's 5-year Wimbledon reign and the beginning of a new chapter.

2013 — Murray Ends the British Wait

Andy Murray became the first British man to win Wimbledon in 77 years, defeating Novak Djokovic in straight sets on a sun-drenched Centre Court. The weight of a nation's expectations finally lifted. Murray fell to his knees, and a country wept with joy. Fred Perry's ghost could finally rest.

2019 — Djokovic Saves Match Points

In a men's final for the ages, Novak Djokovic saved two championship points against Roger Federer at 8-7 in the fifth set, then won the inaugural final-set tiebreak at 12-12. Despite Federer winning more points overall, Djokovic prevailed 13-12 in the fifth. A heartbreaking, extraordinary match that lasted 4 hours and 57 minutes.

2023 — Alcaraz Announces Himself

Carlos Alcaraz, just 20 years old, defeated Novak Djokovic in a five-set final to announce the arrival of tennis's next superstar. The young Spaniard's explosive power, touch, and competitive fire suggested a future Wimbledon great. He backed it up by winning again in 2024.

The Wimbledon Experience

More than a tennis tournament — a British institution.

The Queue

Wimbledon's most unique tradition. Fans queue overnight in Wimbledon Park for daily tickets. Stewards issue queue cards, manage a tented camping area, and enforce the unwritten "Queue etiquette." Approximately 500 ground passes are sold each day. Arrive by 6am for a good chance; overnight for show courts.

Strawberries & Cream

28 tonnes of strawberries and 7,000 litres of cream are consumed during the fortnight. At approximately £2.50 per serving, they're one of sport's most iconic food traditions. Best enjoyed on Henman Hill while watching the big screen.

Henman Hill / Murray Mound

The grassy slope overlooking a giant screen where fans gather to watch matches. Named after Tim Henman (and informally renamed for Andy Murray), it creates a festival atmosphere — cheering, gasping, and groaning in unison. The best free seat at Wimbledon.

The All-White Rule

Players must wear "almost entirely white" — the strictest dress code in professional sport. Even undergarments must be white. The rule has been enforced increasingly strictly, with players sent back to change. It's quintessentially Wimbledon.

The Royal Box

Centre Court's Royal Box seats 74 guests, hosting members of the Royal Family, celebrities, and dignitaries. Players bow or curtsy to the Royal Box if the Prince or Princess of Wales is present — one of the last vestiges of tennis's aristocratic origins.

Pimm's No. 1

The quintessential Wimbledon drink. Approximately 320,000 glasses of Pimm's are served during the tournament. Pimm's, lemonade, strawberries, cucumber, and mint — it tastes like a British summer and pairs perfectly with grass-court tennis.

How to Attend Wimbledon

Getting Tickets

The Ballot: Apply in September for next year's tournament. Random selection for show court tickets at face value (£50-£250). The Queue: Daily tickets sold each morning. Resale: Returned tickets sold at the gate each afternoon. Hospitality: Packages from £500+.

Getting There

Nearest station: Southfields (District line), 15-minute walk. Wimbledon station (District line, Overground, National Rail) with shuttle buses. Parking is extremely limited — public transport is strongly recommended. Allow plenty of time; it's always busier than you think.

When to Go

Week 1 (especially Mon-Wed) offers the best value — more matches, more accessible tickets, and a relaxed atmosphere. The second week is more exclusive but offers higher-quality matches. Finals weekend is the hardest ticket in tennis.

Budget

Ground pass: £25-£50. Show court tickets: £50-£250 (face value), more on resale. Food and drink: £30-£60/day. A day at Wimbledon on a ground pass can cost under £100 — making it one of the most accessible major championships in the world.
