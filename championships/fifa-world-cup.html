FIFA World Cup

The biggest sporting event on planet Earth. Every four years, 211 FIFA nations fight for 48 spots in the tournament that stops the world.

Why It's Tier 1 — Pinnacle

The FIFA World Cup stands alone at the summit of world sport. Here's why nothing else compares.

Global Reach

Every continent, every nation, billions of viewers. The 2022 final reached an estimated 1.5 billion people simultaneously — no other sporting event comes close. FIFA has 211 member associations, more than the United Nations.

Once in a Generation

The four-year cycle makes every World Cup incredibly precious. Players get only 3-4 chances in a career. Entire fan generations are defined by "their" World Cup. It creates urgency and emotional weight that annual events simply cannot match.

Cultural Impact

Countries shut down. Streets empty during matches, then erupt in celebration. Strangers become friends. The World Cup transcends sport entirely — it's a cultural moment that unites entire nations and creates memories that last lifetimes.

Competitive Depth

211 nations enter. Over 800 qualifying matches are played across 2+ years. Only 48 make the finals. The journey from first qualifier to lifting the trophy is the longest and most grueling path in all of sport.

History of the World Cup

94 years of glory, heartbreak, and moments that changed sport forever.

1930–1950: The Birth

The first World Cup was held in Uruguay in 1930, with just 13 teams making the journey by boat across the Atlantic. Uruguay won the inaugural tournament, beating Argentina 4-2 in the final in Montevideo. The tournament was cancelled during World War II, returning in 1950 in Brazil — where Uruguay shocked the hosts in one of sport's most famous upsets, the Maracanazo. Brazil needed only a draw in the final match at the Maracanã in front of 200,000 fans, but Uruguay won 2-1, plunging an entire nation into mourning.

1958–1970: The Pelé Era

A 17-year-old Pelé burst onto the world stage in Sweden in 1958, scoring twice in the final as Brazil won their first World Cup. Brazil won again in 1962 (Chile) and then produced arguably the greatest football team ever assembled at Mexico 1970 — Pelé, Jairzinho, Tostão, Rivelino, and Carlos Alberto. Their 4-1 demolition of Italy in the final, including Carlos Alberto's unforgettable team goal, remains the gold standard of beautiful football. England had their moment too, winning the 1966 World Cup at Wembley with Geoff Hurst's hat-trick and Kenneth Wolstenholme's immortal words: "They think it's all over... it is now!"

1974–1990: Total Football to Maradona

The Netherlands' "Total Football" under Rinus Michels and Johan Cruyff revolutionized the game at the 1974 World Cup in West Germany, though they fell short in the final. Argentina won controversially at home in 1978 amid political turmoil. Then came Diego Maradona. At Mexico 1986, Maradona put on the greatest individual World Cup performance in history — including both the infamous "Hand of God" and the "Goal of the Century" against England in the quarter-final. He dragged Argentina to the title almost single-handedly. West Germany's efficiency shone through in 1990 Italy, beating Maradona's Argentina in a tense final.

1994–2002: Globalization

The 1994 World Cup in the USA brought football to America — and it worked, with record attendances. Brazil won their fourth title. France's golden generation won at home in 1998, with Zinedine Zidane scoring twice in the final against Brazil. In 2002, the tournament headed to Asia for the first time (Japan and South Korea), and Ronaldo — who had suffered a mysterious seizure before the 1998 final — found redemption, scoring both goals in Brazil's 2-0 victory over Germany to claim a record fifth World Cup.

2006–2018: The Modern Era

Germany 2006 is remembered for Zidane's headbutt on Materazzi in the final — a surreal end to a legendary career, with Italy winning on penalties. Spain's tiki-taka revolution peaked in South Africa 2010, winning their first World Cup with Iniesta's extra-time goal. Brazil 2014 produced the most shocking result in World Cup history — Germany's 7-1 semi-final demolition of the hosts, the Mineirazo. Then in Russia 2018, a young French team led by 19-year-old Kylian Mbappé won the title with exhilarating attacking football.

2022: The Greatest Final Ever

Qatar 2022 delivered the most dramatic World Cup final in history. Argentina, led by Lionel Messi in his final World Cup, raced to a 2-0 lead through Messi's penalty and Ángel Di María's sublime team goal. France looked finished. Then Kylian Mbappé scored twice in 97 seconds — a penalty and a stunning volley — to force extra time at 2-2. Messi scored again to make it 3-2, but Mbappé completed his hat-trick from the penalty spot. Argentina won the shootout 4-2, and Messi — at 35, in his fifth World Cup — finally lifted the trophy that had eluded him his entire career. It was the perfect ending to the greatest rivalry in modern football: Messi vs Mbappé, Argentina vs France, destiny vs defiance.

How the World Cup Works

From first qualifier to lifting the trophy — the longest journey in sport.

Qualification (2+ Years)

All 211 FIFA member nations can enter qualification. Over 800 matches are played across 6 confederations: UEFA (Europe), CONMEBOL (South America), CONCACAF (North/Central America & Caribbean), CAF (Africa), AFC (Asia), and OFC (Oceania). Each confederation has a different format — from round-robin leagues to two-legged playoffs. Only 48 teams (from 2026) make it through.

Group Stage

In the 2026 format, 48 teams are divided into 12 groups of 4. Each team plays 3 group matches. The top 2 in each group plus the 8 best third-placed teams advance to the knockout rounds — 32 teams total progressing from the group stage.

Knockout Rounds

Single-elimination matches. Round of 32 → Round of 16 → Quarter-finals → Semi-finals → Third-place play-off → Final. If a knockout match is tied after 90 minutes, 30 minutes of extra time is played. If still tied, the match goes to a penalty shootout.

The Final

A single match at a pre-selected stadium. The culmination of 4 years of football, watched by 1.5+ billion people worldwide. The winning captain lifts the FIFA World Cup Trophy. It is the single most-watched, most emotionally charged moment in world sport.

2026 World Cup Preview

The biggest World Cup ever is coming to North America.

The 2026 FIFA World Cup will be the first with 48 teams, hosted across three nations — the United States, Canada, and Mexico. It will be the largest, most ambitious World Cup in history, with matches played in 16 host cities across the continent.

Iconic Moments

The moments that define the World Cup — and sport itself.

1950 — The Maracanazo

Uruguay shock hosts Brazil 2-1 in the deciding match at the Maracanã in front of nearly 200,000 fans. The silence that followed is described as the loudest silence in sporting history.

1966 — "They Think It's All Over"

Geoff Hurst scores a hat-trick as England beat West Germany 4-2 in the final at Wembley. Kenneth Wolstenholme's commentary — "They think it's all over... it is now!" — becomes the most famous line in British sport.

1970 — The Beautiful Game Goal

Brazil's 4th goal in the 1970 final against Italy — a flowing team move finished by captain Carlos Alberto — is considered the greatest team goal in history. This Brazil side is often called the best football team ever assembled.

1986 — Maradona vs England

Two goals that defined a career and a rivalry. First, the "Hand of God" — a deliberate handball that the referee missed. Then, minutes later, the "Goal of the Century" — Maradona dribbles past five England players from the halfway line and slots home. Genius and villainy in the same 10 minutes.

1998 — Zidane's Final

Zinedine Zidane scores two headers in the World Cup final as France beat Brazil 3-0 on home soil. A nation celebrates on the Champs-Élysées. Zidane, of Algerian descent, becomes the symbol of multicultural France.

2014 — The Mineirazo

Germany 7, Brazil 1. In Belo Horizonte. In a World Cup semi-final. In Brazil's own World Cup. Five goals in 18 first-half minutes. Brazilian fans weeping in the stands. The most shocking result in World Cup history.

2022 — Messi's Coronation

The greatest final in history. Argentina 3-3 France after extra time, with a Mbappé hat-trick and two Messi goals. Argentina win on penalties. Lionel Messi, at 35, in his 5th and final World Cup, lifts the one trophy that had eluded him. The GOAT debate is settled — at least for his supporters — in the most dramatic way imaginable.

How to Attend the World Cup

Your guide to experiencing the world's greatest sporting event in person.

Tickets

Sold through FIFA.com in ballot phases 12-18 months before the tournament. Group stage: ~$70-$300. Final: ~$600+. Hospitality packages from $1,000+. Never buy from unofficial sources.

Budget

Plan for $3,000-$10,000+ per person for a multi-match trip. Biggest costs: flights, accommodation (prices surge 3-5x during the tournament), and tickets. Budget travelers can spend less by attending group stages only.

Visa & Travel

For 2026: US requires ESTA or visa, Canada requires eTA, Mexico is visa-free for many nationalities. Book flights 6-12 months early. Consider basing yourself in one city to minimize travel costs.

Accommodation

Hotels book up fast and prices surge. Consider Airbnb, hostels, or staying slightly outside host cities. For 2026, the US has ample accommodation, but NYC, LA, and Miami will still fill up quickly.

Fan Fests

FIFA Fan Fests are free outdoor events in every host city with big screens, live music, food, and merchandise. They're the best free experience at the World Cup — especially if you can't get match tickets.

What to Bring

Team jersey, comfortable shoes, portable charger, sunscreen, rain jacket, ID/passport, printed tickets, and an open heart. The World Cup is about the people as much as the football.

Frequently Asked Questions

The FIFA World Cup is the premier international football (soccer) tournament in the world. Organized by FIFA, it brings together national teams from across the globe every four years to compete for the most coveted trophy in sport. First held in 1930 in Uruguay, the World Cup has grown into the single most-watched sporting event on Earth, with the 2022 final between Argentina and France drawing an estimated 1.5 billion viewers worldwide. It is rated Tier 1 (Pinnacle) in the ChampionshipsGuide system.

The FIFA World Cup is held every four years. The most recent edition was in 2022 (Qatar), and the next will be in 2026 (USA, Canada, Mexico). The four-year cycle makes each tournament incredibly precious — players may only get 3-4 chances in a career.

Starting from the 2026 World Cup, the tournament features 48 teams, expanded from 32. All 211 FIFA member associations are eligible to enter qualification. The expanded format means more nations than ever will experience the World Cup finals.

The 2026 FIFA World Cup will be hosted jointly by the United States, Canada, and Mexico across 16 host cities including New York/New Jersey, Los Angeles, Miami, Dallas, Houston, Atlanta, Seattle, San Francisco, Toronto, Vancouver, Mexico City, and more.

Brazil holds the record with 5 World Cup victories (1958, 1962, 1970, 1994, 2002). Germany and Italy follow with 4 each, Argentina has 3, and France and Uruguay have 2 each. England and Spain have won once.

World Cup tickets are sold primarily through FIFA.com in several sales phases beginning 1-2 years before the tournament. Distribution is via a ballot (lottery) system, with additional first-come-first-served phases. Prices range from ~$70 for group stage to $600+ for the final. FIFA also offers hospitality packages.

The 2022 final between Argentina and France is widely considered the greatest World Cup final. Argentina led 2-0, Mbappé scored twice in 97 seconds to make it 2-2, Messi scored again in extra time, Mbappé completed his hat-trick from the spot, and Argentina won 4-2 on penalties. Lionel Messi's crowning moment.

Attending the FIFA World Cup typically costs between $3,000 and $10,000+ per person depending on how many matches you attend, accommodation choices, and the host country. Budget includes tickets ($70-$600+ each), flights, accommodation ($100-$400+/night), food and transport ($50-$150/day).
