Women's Championships

A New Era

The Golden Age of Women's Sport

We are living through the most exciting era in the history of women's sport. What was once an afterthought has become a movement — one backed by record-breaking attendance figures, unprecedented broadcast deals, surging prize money, and a cultural shift that recognizes women's championships as world-class sporting events in their own right.

The numbers tell a powerful story. The 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup drew over 2 million in-stadium spectators across Australia and New Zealand, shattering previous records. The 2022 Women's Euro final at Wembley Stadium was attended by 87,192 fans — the largest crowd for any European Championship match, men's or women's, in history. The 2024 WNBA season delivered record television viewership, driven by a new generation of superstars and a surge of mainstream media attention.

Prize money is rising, too. All four tennis Grand Slams now offer equal prize money for men's and women's singles champions. Cricket's Women's Premier League (WPL) launched in 2023 with franchise fees that stunned the cricketing world. The NWSL has signed media deals worth hundreds of millions of dollars. FIFA has committed to closing the prize money gap between the men's and women's World Cups.

But this golden age is about more than money or viewership. It is about the growing recognition that women's championships produce drama, athleticism, and moments of brilliance that stand alongside anything in men's sport. The 2019 US Women's World Cup final was the most-watched football match in American television history. Simone Biles's Olympic performances have transcended sport to become cultural events. The rise of the WNBA, women's cricket, and women's football leagues across Europe represents a fundamental and permanent shift in the sporting landscape.

This page is our celebration of that shift. Below, you will find every major women's championship organized by tier, along with rising events, historic milestones, and everything you need to know about women's sport at its highest level.

Tier 1

Pinnacle Women's Championships

These are the absolute pinnacle of women's competitive sport. Global audiences in the billions, participation from nations across every continent, and cultural significance that transcends sport. Winning a Tier 1 women's championship means etching your name into history forever.

Tier 1

Football

FIFA Women's World Cup

The pinnacle of women's football and one of the biggest sporting events on the planet. Thirty-two national teams compete every four years in a month-long global tournament. The 2023 edition in Australia and New Zealand set records with over 2 million in-stadium spectators, cementing the Women's World Cup as a Tier 1 championship alongside the men's event. The United States are the most successful team with four titles. The next edition will be held in Brazil in 2027.

🌐 32 Teams — International Tournament 📅 Jul–Aug (every 4 years)
Tier 1

Multi-sport

Women's Summer Olympic Games

Women's events at the Summer Olympics hold the same Tier 1 status as men's. From track and field and gymnastics to swimming, basketball, football, and tennis, women's Olympic competition features the world's finest athletes representing their nations on the biggest stage. Since 2012, every Olympic sport includes women's events. The Olympic Games unite over 200 nations and billions of viewers, and winning an Olympic gold medal remains the highest honor in women's sport.

🏅 Multi-sport Festival 📅 Jul–Aug (every 4 years)
Tier 1

Multi-sport

Women's Winter Olympic Games

Women's winter sports at the Olympic Games showcase extraordinary athleticism and courage. Alpine skiing, figure skating, cross-country skiing, snowboarding, speed skating, freestyle skiing, biathlon, and more — women's Winter Olympic events produce some of the most thrilling and visually spectacular moments in all of sport. Athletes like Mikaela Shiffrin, Lindsey Vonn, and Yuzuru Hanyu's female counterparts have become household names through the global platform of the Winter Games.

🏅 Multi-sport Festival 📅 Feb (every 4 years)
Tier 2

Elite Women's Championships

The highest annual and seasonal women's championships. These events command massive audiences, feature the world's best female athletes, and have established themselves as must-watch sporting events in their own right.

Tier 2

Football

UEFA Women's Champions League

The pinnacle of European women's club football. The UEFA Women's Champions League brings together the best clubs from across the continent in an annual competition that has grown exponentially in prestige and viewership. FC Barcelona and Olympique Lyonnais have dominated in recent years, with Lyon winning the title a record eight times. The 2022 final at Camp Nou drew over 91,000 fans — a record for a women's club match. The competition continues to expand, with growing broadcast deals and increased investment transforming the landscape of women's club football.

⚽ Club Competition 📅 Sep–May (annual)
Tier 2

Tennis

WTA Finals

The season-ending championship of women's tennis, featuring the top 8 singles players and the top 8 doubles teams in the world. The WTA Finals is the pinnacle of the women's tennis calendar, bringing together the best female players for a week of elite round-robin competition followed by knockout semifinals and a final. The tournament boasts a rich history stretching back to 1972, with legends like Serena Williams, Martina Navratilova, Steffi Graf, and Chris Evert among its champions. It is the definitive year-end test of who is the best player on the planet.

🎾 Year-End Championship 📅 November (annual)
Tier 2

Tennis

Wimbledon Women's Singles

The most prestigious individual title in women's tennis. Played on the immaculate grass courts of the All England Club in London since 1884, the Wimbledon Women's Singles is the crown jewel of the Grand Slams. With its all-white dress code, royal patronage, and unmatched tradition, lifting the Venus Rosewater Dish on Centre Court is the ultimate achievement for any female tennis player. Martina Navratilova holds the record with nine singles titles. Equal prize money has been awarded since 2007.

🎾 Grand Slam 📅 Jun–Jul (annual)
Tier 2

Cricket

ICC Women's Cricket World Cup

The pinnacle of women's international cricket. Held every four years, the ICC Women's Cricket World Cup has grown from a niche event into a globally broadcast tournament with significant viewership across cricket-playing nations. Australia are the most dominant force in women's cricket history, but the competition has become increasingly competitive, with India, England, New Zealand, and South Africa all challenging for the title. The tournament features eight teams in a round-robin format followed by knockout rounds.

🏏 International Tournament 📅 Feb–Mar (every 4 years)
Tier 2

Rugby Union

Women's Rugby World Cup

The global championship of women's rugby union. New Zealand's Black Ferns have been the dominant force, winning six of the first nine tournaments, but the gap is closing rapidly. England, France, Canada, and the United States have all emerged as serious contenders. The 2022 tournament in New Zealand drew record attendance and viewership, and the growing professionalization of women's rugby is producing increasingly competitive and compelling matches at the highest level.

🏉 International Tournament 📅 Oct–Nov (every 4 years)
Tier 2

Basketball

WNBA Finals

The championship series of the world's premier women's professional basketball league. The WNBA has experienced a massive surge in popularity, with record viewership and attendance driven by generational talents and growing cultural significance. The Las Vegas Aces, Seattle Storm, Minnesota Lynx, and Phoenix Mercury have been among the league's most successful franchises. The best-of-five finals series determines the WNBA champion each October, producing dramatic basketball that has captivated an expanding global audience.

🏀 League Final 📅 October (annual)
Tier 3

Major Women's Championships

Established championships with significant audiences, strong competitive fields, and growing cultural importance. These events are essential pillars of the women's sporting landscape.

Tier 3

Women's US Open

Tennis

The final Grand Slam of the calendar year, held at the USTA Billie Jean King National Tennis Center in New York. The US Open was the first Grand Slam to offer equal prize money for men and women in 1973. Its electric night sessions under the New York lights create an unforgettable atmosphere.

Tier 3

Women's French Open

Tennis

Roland-Garros is the world's premier clay-court tournament. The women's draw produces incredible champions — from Chris Evert's seven titles to Justine Henin's four and Iga Swiatek's modern dominance. The slow red clay demands exceptional fitness, patience, and tactical intelligence.

Tier 3

Women's Australian Open

Tennis

The first Grand Slam of the year, held in Melbourne every January. Known as the "Happy Slam," the Australian Open has been a launchpad for some of women's tennis's greatest champions. Serena Williams won seven titles here, and Margaret Court holds the all-time record with eleven.

Tier 3

Women's Six Nations

Rugby Union

The annual European women's rugby championship featuring England, France, Ireland, Italy, Scotland, and Wales. England's Red Roses have been dominant, but France and Ireland consistently challenge. The tournament has grown rapidly in attendance and broadcast coverage, with matches now regularly televised across Europe.

Tier 3

Women's T20 World Cup

Cricket

The global championship for women's T20 cricket. The fast-paced, explosive format has attracted new audiences to women's cricket, with the 2020 final at the MCG in Melbourne drawing 86,174 spectators — a record for any women's cricket match. Australia have been the dominant force, but India and England are closing the gap.

Tier 3

NWSL Championship

Football

The championship of the National Women's Soccer League, the top professional women's football league in the United States. The NWSL has experienced massive growth, fueled by record-breaking media deals, expansion teams, and the star power of US Women's National Team players. The league attracts top international talent from around the world.

Tier 3

Women's FA Cup

Football

England's premier women's knockout cup competition. The Women's FA Cup has grown enormously in stature, with finals at Wembley Stadium drawing large crowds and significant television audiences. Chelsea, Arsenal, and Manchester City have been the dominant forces in recent years.

Tier 3

Women's EHF Champions League

Handball

The premier club competition in European women's handball. Featuring the top clubs from across Europe, the Women's EHF Champions League showcases incredible athleticism, speed, and tactical complexity. Scandinavian and Eastern European clubs have traditionally dominated, with Gyori Audi ETO KC and Vipers Kristiansand among recent champions.

Tier 3

LPGA Tour Championship

Golf

The season-ending championship of the Ladies Professional Golf Association, featuring the top players on the LPGA Tour. The LPGA Tour Championship is the culmination of a global season that spans dozens of tournaments across multiple continents. It crowns the best female golfer of the year.

Tier 3

Solheim Cup

Golf

The women's equivalent of the Ryder Cup, pitting the best female golfers from Europe against the United States in a biennial team match-play competition. The Solheim Cup has grown into one of the most passionate and competitive events in women's golf, with electric atmospheres and intense national pride on both sides.

Tier 3

Women's Hockey World Cup

Field Hockey

The global championship of women's field hockey, organized by FIH every four years. The Netherlands have been the most successful nation, with a passionate following across Europe, Oceania, and Asia. Women's field hockey is one of the most-watched women's sports at the Olympic Games, and the World Cup is its standalone showcase event.

History

Records & Milestones

The key moments that have defined the rise of women's championships. From record-breaking crowds to equal prize money, these milestones mark the path to the golden age.

1999

US Women's World Cup Final — 90,185 at the Rose Bowl

The 1999 FIFA Women's World Cup final between the United States and China drew 90,185 spectators to the Rose Bowl in Pasadena, California — a record attendance for a women's sporting event at the time. Brandi Chastain's iconic penalty kick celebration became one of the most famous images in sports history. This moment is widely regarded as the birth of the modern era of women's sport.

2007

Equal Prize Money at All Four Tennis Grand Slams

Wimbledon became the last of the four Grand Slam tournaments to offer equal prize money for men's and women's singles champions. The US Open led the way in 1973, followed by the Australian Open in 2001 and the French Open in 2006. Tennis's commitment to equal pay has made it a benchmark for other sports.

2012

Every Olympic Sport Includes Women

The 2012 London Olympics marked a historic milestone: for the first time in Olympic history, every sport on the program included women's events. Women's boxing made its Olympic debut, completing the inclusion. Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and Brunei also sent female athletes to the Olympics for the first time. The London Games were dubbed "the Women's Olympics" for the unprecedented prominence of women's sport.

2019

US Women's World Cup Final — Most-Watched Football Game in US History

The 2019 FIFA Women's World Cup final between the United States and the Netherlands drew 14.3 million viewers on Fox in the United States, making it the most-watched football (soccer) match in American television history at the time — surpassing all men's World Cup matches. Globally, the tournament reached over 1.1 billion viewers, demonstrating the massive and growing global appetite for women's football.

2022

Women's Euro Final at Wembley — 87,192 Attendance

The UEFA Women's Euro 2022 final between England and Germany at Wembley Stadium drew 87,192 spectators — the largest attendance for any European Championship match, men's or women's, in history. England's 2-1 victory was watched by a peak television audience of 17.4 million in the UK, making it the most-watched women's football match in British history. The tournament was a watershed moment for women's football in Europe.

2023

Women's World Cup Sets New Attendance Records

The 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup in Australia and New Zealand broke multiple attendance records, with over 2 million total in-stadium spectators across the tournament. The event was broadcast to an estimated 2 billion viewers worldwide, cementing the Women's World Cup as one of the most significant sporting events on the planet. Spain won their first Women's World Cup title, and the tournament showcased the growing depth and quality of women's football globally.

2024

Record WNBA Viewership

The 2024 WNBA season delivered record television viewership, driven by a new generation of superstars entering the league and unprecedented mainstream media attention. Average viewership across ESPN, ABC, and CBS broadcasts reached all-time highs. The league's expansion, new media rights deal, and growing global fanbase cemented the WNBA's position as one of the premier women's sports leagues in the world. The moment reflected a broader cultural shift toward serious engagement with women's professional basketball.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Clear answers to the most common questions about women's championships across every sport.

The FIFA Women's World Cup has the largest global audience of any women's championship. The 2023 tournament, held across Australia and New Zealand, drew over 2 million in-stadium spectators and was broadcast to an estimated 2 billion viewers worldwide. The 2019 Women's World Cup final between the USA and the Netherlands was the most-watched women's football match in history at the time, with over 1.1 billion viewers globally.

The Women's Olympic Games also command massive audiences, particularly in events like gymnastics, swimming, and track and field. The WNBA has experienced record viewership growth in 2024, driven by generational talents entering the league.

Prize money equality varies significantly across sports. Tennis has led the way — all four Grand Slam tournaments (Australian Open, French Open, Wimbledon, and US Open) now offer equal prize money for men's and women's singles champions. The US Open was the first to achieve this in 1973, and Wimbledon was the last to equalize in 2007.

In football, a significant gap remains: the total prize pool for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup was $152 million compared to $440 million for the 2022 Men's World Cup, though FIFA has committed to closing this gap. The WNBA has made strides with new media deals, and cricket has seen investment growth through the Women's Premier League (WPL). Overall, while the trend is firmly toward greater equality, most women's championships still offer lower prize money than their men's equivalents.

The FIFA Women's World Cup is the biggest women's football championship in the world. Held every four years since 1991, it is rated Tier 1 (Pinnacle) in the ChampionshipsGuide system — the same tier as the men's FIFA World Cup. The tournament features 32 national teams competing across a month-long event. The 2023 edition in Australia and New Zealand set new records with over 2 million in-stadium spectators. The next Women's World Cup will be held in Brazil in 2027.

At the club level, the UEFA Women's Champions League is the most prestigious competition, featuring top European clubs like FC Barcelona and Olympique Lyonnais. The NWSL Championship in the United States and the Women's FA Cup in England are also major women's football championships.

Women's cricket has experienced extraordinary growth over the past decade. The ICC Women's Cricket World Cup, held every four years, has grown from a relatively niche event to a globally broadcast tournament with significant viewership, particularly in cricket-strong nations like Australia, England, India, and New Zealand.

The most transformative development has been the launch of women's franchise leagues — most notably the Women's Premier League (WPL) in India, launched in 2023, which has brought unprecedented investment, media attention, and professionalism to the women's game. The Women's Big Bash League in Australia has also been a trailblazer, operating since 2015 and helping develop world-class players. The ICC Women's T20 World Cup has grown rapidly, and bilateral series between top nations now receive significant broadcast coverage.

Several women's championships offer outstanding live experiences. The FIFA Women's World Cup is the ultimate event — the 2023 tournament in Australia and New Zealand featured packed stadiums with electric atmospheres. The Wimbledon Women's Singles Final at Centre Court offers an intimate yet prestigious experience with the traditions of the All England Club.

The WNBA Finals have grown into a must-attend event, with passionate fanbases creating incredible arena atmospheres. The UEFA Women's Champions League Final has drawn record crowds — the 2022 final at Camp Nou attracted over 91,000 fans. The Women's Six Nations in rugby offers great value with accessible ticket prices and authentic, passionate crowds. For individual sports, the WTA Finals bring together the top eight players in the world for an exclusive, high-quality tennis experience.

The next FIFA Women's World Cup will be held in Brazil in 2027. This will be the first time Brazil hosts the Women's World Cup, and it promises to be a landmark event for women's football in South America.

The 2027 tournament will build on the record-breaking success of the 2023 Women's World Cup in Australia and New Zealand, which drew over 2 million in-stadium spectators and was broadcast to billions worldwide. Qualification for the 2027 tournament will take place through each confederation's qualifying process throughout 2025 and 2026. The tournament is expected to feature 32 teams and will be played across multiple Brazilian cities.
